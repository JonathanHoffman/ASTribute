Aaron Swartz

Programmer. Entrepreneur. Activist.

November 8, 1986 - January 11 2013

Timeline of Aaron Swartz's life:

  • 1986 - Born in Highland Park, Illinois
  • 1999 - Wins the ArsDigita Prize, given to young people who create "useful, educational, and collaborative" websites
  • 2000 - Starts work on the RSS 1.0 web syndication standard
  • 2005 - Joins the very first Y Combinator Summer Founders Program working on his website Infogami.
  • 2006 - After Infogami merged with Reddit, Reddit was acquired by Conde Nast Publications and Swartz moves to San Fransisco.
  • 2007 - Co-founds a new firm Jottit, once again working for the goal of making visually intriguing websites easy with Markdown.
  • 2008 - Pursuing his passion for activism, Swartz founds Watchdog.net. Also writes Guerilla Open Access Manifesto.
  • 2009 - Launches the Progressive Campaign Change Committee. This organization explored progressive activism. As Swartz wrote on his blog "I spend my days experimenting with new ways to get progressive policies enacted and progressive politicians elected."
  • 2010 - Founds Demand Progress, a political advocacy group that organizes people online to "take action by contacting Congress and other leaders, funding pressure tactics, and spreading the word" about civil liberties, government reform, and other issues.
  • 2011 - Working in Harvard University's Edmond J. Safra Research Lab on Institutional Corruption, Swartz researches political corruption.
  • 2012 - Leads the campaign against SOPA. Swartz was the keynote speaker at the FC2: Freedom to Connect event in Washington, DC., following the defeat of the bill. "We won this fight because everyone made themselves the hero of their own story. Everyone took it as their job to save this crucial freedom."
  • 2013 - Found dead of suicide in his Brooklyn apartment. Swartz was facing disproportionate charges stemming from his 2008 actions where he downloaded public domain academic articles from the pay-walled PACER database. The articles were later published.
  • 2013 - The first implementation of Aaron Swartz's DeadDrop project (called StrongBox at the time, later renamed to SecureDrop) is launched at The New Yorker.
